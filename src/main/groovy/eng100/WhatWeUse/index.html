Learning About Screws

Creation Date: Sept. 24, 2012

Screw Threads

Let's start with what makes a screw unique in hardware: the threads. The threads from the screw make the screw unique because the screw is one of the only pieces of hardware that contains threads and anything else that has threads can usually be classified as a screw. The threads for a screw have two main functions. One of the functions the thread for a screw serves is converting rotational motion to linear motion. Some examples of this are vehicles that move from screws, fastening, measurement, worm drives, and conveying items from one place to another. Another function of screw threads is to prevent linear motion in the opposite direction unless the screw threads are being rotated in the according direction. This is how fastening things with screws works. When a screw turns, it will hold the two objects being fastened together until the screw is turned in the opposite direction it was fastened with.

Screw threads can be classfied in a variety of ways. One standard that screw threads can be classified under is the UTS standard (United Thread Standard). UTS standard is most commonly used in the United States and Canada. UTS describes the screw thread by puting the size of the hole the screw can be inserted into, a colon, then puting how many threads per inch the screw has (hole size:threads per inch). Another standard that is commonly used in the United States is the ISO metric screw thread standard. This standard is used by puting an M, the outer diameter in millimeters, a multiplication sign, then how far the screw moves in millimeters by rotating the head a full turn (Example: M5*1).

Screw Sizes

Screw sizes have multiple dimensions. Gauge refers to the diameter of the screw and is written simply as a number starting with 0 being the smallest gauge referring to the smallest diameter. The next measurement is the number of threads per inch. The fewer threads per in the faster the screw is driven. The final measurement used is simply the length of the screw. Length is measured from where the screw will lay level with the material it is driven into to the tip. The standard fromat for the measurement for a screw is Gauge - TPI (Threads per inch) X Length. A #8 (8 gauge) screw with 32 threads per inch that is one inch long would be listed as 8-32 X 1". Many times the threads per inch measurement is dropped so the aforementioned screw would be 8-32" The other common specification for a screw refers to the driver needed to turn the screw. The most common types of drives are phillips and regular. This is an indented patter at the top of the screw where a tool is inserted to turn the screw.

Drills

Drills are devices used to bore holes in materials. They use screws inorder to aid in the boring of the hole. The thread pattern on the drills brings the material up to the surface. Drills are also very low effort tools since most of the force is spent turning the screw rather than forcing it through the material. This is again aided by the threads which drive the drill further and further down or through the material as it turns.

Pan head
A low disc with chamfered outer edge

Button or dome head
Cylindrical with a rounded top

Round head
A dome-shaped head used for decoration.

Mushroom or Truss head
Lower-profile dome designed to prevent tampering

Countersunk or flat head
Conical, with flat outer face and tapering inner face allowing it to sink into the material.

Oval or raised head
A decorative screw head with a countersunk bottom and rounded top. Also known as "raised countersunk"

Bugle head
Similar to countersunk, but there is a smooth progression from the shank to the angle of the head, similar to the bell of a bugle.

Cheese head
Disc with cylindrical outer edge, height approximately half the head diameter

Fillister head
Cylindrical, but with a slightly convex top surface. Height to diameter ratio is larger than cheese head.

Flanged head
A flanged head can be any of the above head styles (except the countersunk styles) with the addition of an integrated flange at the base of the head.

Grades

Grade refers to the strength of the screw. The metric system and inch system have different grade ratings, but for both the higher the number the greater the strength. The grade rating is stamped on the head or on the box that the screw comes in. In the metric system the numbers are cla ssified with a first number and a single digit decimal number. The first number refers to the ultimate tensile strength, or the minimum stress the bolt can withstand before breaking. The second number refers to the tensile yield strength, or the strength that the bolt or screw cannot not recover from. In the Inch system, the numbers fo grade are specified as a single digit. The are classified on the SAEJ429 classification system for strength. The numbers range from 0 to 8, with 8 being the strongest. There are no grades above 8 for this classification. The most common used grades are 5 and 8.
